1: Why Finance?

Quantitative finance is the opposite of psychological economics, it aims to analyse the economy mathematically using algorithmic approaches and models. It stems from the notion that stock prices are essentially random in their fluctuations but can be modelled and by extension predicted. Financial theory defines a set of rules and guidelines governing traditional finance.

Index - a collection of data gathered over a period of time.

Leverage - using a small sum to generate a larger sum through various methods.

Principle Guarentee - assurance that no money will be lost once loaned.

Efficient Market Theory - the idea that the market will balance itself if allowed to trade freely and openly.

Monopoly - singular control of an entire sector (leads to instability).

Financial systems are used to transfer wealth between states via competitive promises and to enforce those promises. The enforcement is critical as without it, the promises are worthless.

An economics model is simply a mathematical representation of a trading scenario. Each model consists of a series of equations which describe relationships between entities.

Question: Why is the free market so good?

Diminishing Market Utility - when the value of each successive good decreases in worth to the person aquiring it (quadratic or logarithmic welfare function).

The free market enables equilibrium to occur, maximising the sum of utilities. Having diminishing market utility means when dy/dx = 0, it implies the maximum of x has been reached. If the margin utilities are the same, euilibrium does not maximise the sum of utilities.

An important discovery as a result of the above equations was the indifference curve developed by Edgeworth. It visualises the effect equilibrium has on the ratio of goods each individual posesses, for example a person may be happy with 2 oranges and 1 apple but equally happy with 1 orange and 2 apples, if they are worth the same. The equations above can be used to find the position where curves overlap i.e. when both people have as much of all goods as possible (equilibrium).

The calculations above all form part of the first theorem of welfare economics and were used to calculate the general equilibrium of the economy. General equilibrium (theory) attempts to explain supply, demand and prices in any market and results in a single 'general' equilibrium.

There are several flaws with general equilibrium, mainly the justice based argument - that is it does not factor in the social fairness of the allocations as it obeys Pareto efficiency. Other critical weaknesses include lack of adjustments for externalities, in this model people do not affect eachother's behaviour and dependencies on external things are not accounted for. Another critical weakness is that diminishing margin utility isn't always true (think the value of 1 shoe compared to 2), and it assumes perfect competition which doesn't happen in the real world. It does give a good foundation on which to build more complex models, including financial models.

Financial Model - a mathematical model targeting financial assets and other financial factors.

Converting an economics model (as in earlier lessons) into a financial model requires several modifications.

  • Add time as a factor
  • Add risk and its effects on trading
  • Include money with interest rates and credit
  • Include banks and institutions roles
  • Add conditions for collateral and defaults
  • Add assets

Incorporating time into the model requires the least modification (none). Instead think of x as any given good and y as the same good an amount of time later. The amount of time is up to you but any financially relevant increment makes sense (3 months/ 12 months etc.).

Assets are more difficult to implement as they require their own variables, here we introduce D for both x and y representing the current payment and the future payment of any asset. These new values affect Px and Py along with introducing several more variables as we will go on to discuss.

5: Present Value Prices and the Real Rate of Interest

The price of any good depends on its margin utility, with a high margin utility the good will keep more of its value over time (can be higher than 1), with a low margin utility the good depreciates in value with time.

Present Value Price - the price of something if you were to buy it today i.e. using today's currency.
